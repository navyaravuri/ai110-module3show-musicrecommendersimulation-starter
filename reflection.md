# Reflection

## Why did High-Energy Rock and Deep Intense Rock get such similar results?

Both profiles are essentially asking for the same thing sonically: loud, fast, low-acoustic music. The main difference is that Deep Intense Rock pushed every dial further — higher energy, faster tempo, and an angrier mood target. But because there is only one rock song in the entire catalog (Storm Runner), it shows up at #1 for both profiles no matter what. It's like going to a restaurant that only has one pasta dish and ordering either "pasta" or "spicy pasta" — you're getting the same plate either way. The deeper problem is that once Storm Runner claims the genre bonus, the remaining four spots are filled by songs from completely different genres just because they happen to be loud and fast. The system is doing its best, but the catalog doesn't give it enough rock songs to work with.

## Why did removing mood break the Chill Lofi results?

When mood matching was switched off, the top result for the Chill Lofi profile became Focus Flow — a lofi song tagged as "focused," not "chill." To a human ear those feel different: focused music is what you put on to concentrate, chill music is what you put on to wind down. The system couldn't tell the difference anymore because mood was the only signal it had for that distinction. Genre told it "this is lofi," but without mood it had no way of knowing whether the user wanted to study or relax. The results weren't completely wrong — Focus Flow is still a lofi track — but the recommendation lost its emotional accuracy. It was like a friend recommending study music when you asked for something to listen to in the bath.

## Why did the Reggae profile still return reasonable results despite no matching songs?

Even without a single reggae song in the catalog, the system didn't crash or return garbage — it fell back to mood and numeric similarity. Focus Flow came up first because it matched the "focused" mood target and had similar energy and tempo to what the profile was describing. The results felt like a reasonable second opinion: "we don't have what you asked for, but here are some things in the same neighborhood." The issue is that the system never told the user this was happening. The scores still showed out of 9.0, even though the actual ceiling was 7.0 since the genre bonus could never fire. It's like a store showing a product as 70% off when the full price was never available — the number is accurate but the context is missing.

## Why was the Classical adversarial profile the most broken result?

The Classical adversarial profile was designed to ask for something impossible: classical music with the energy of a metal song. The one classical song in the catalog — Midnight Sonata — is a quiet, slow piano piece. When tested, it still came out #1 because the genre label alone was worth enough points to drag it past Blackout Riff, which was actually a near-perfect numeric match for every target the user described. The margin was just 0.06 points. This reveals something important about how the scoring works: the genre label is treated as a strong signal of identity, almost like a genre stamp of approval, even when everything else about the song contradicts what the user wants. In a real music app, this would feel like Spotify recommending a lullaby to someone who asked for workout music — just because both are in the "classical" playlist.

## What did running the weight experiments reveal?

Two experiments were run to test whether adjusting the numbers could fix these problems. First, the energy weight was doubled and the genre weight was halved. This fixed the Classical adversarial problem — Midnight Sonata dropped out of #1 — but it broke the rock profiles because Storm Runner lost to Blackout Riff even for users who explicitly wanted rock music. Turning one dial fixed one thing and broke another. The second experiment removed mood matching entirely. Without it, the system still found songs, but they felt emotionally disconnected from what each profile was describing. A user asking for chill lofi got a study-mode lofi song. A user with no matching genre got results that had nothing to do with their stated mood. Both experiments pointed to the same conclusion: the weights are reasonably balanced as they are, and the real limitation is that 18 songs is just not enough data for a recommender to work well.
